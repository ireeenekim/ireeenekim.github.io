Ulises Carrión — Mail Art and the Big Monster from Second Thoughts

I found the idea that mail serves as the support rather than the medium of mail art especially interesting. At first, I was more focused on the “mail” itself, but the author’s examples—like a gift where delivery is central versus a bank letter where content is more central—helped clarify his distinction between “mail” vs. “art” and helped me better understand how mail operates within the framework of mail art. After reading the whole piece, it also made me reflect on how the internet can both expand and disrupt mail art, altering its tangible and experiential qualities.

Discussion Questions
  • Do you agree with Carrión's take that mail art has "Very little to do with mail and a lot to do with art?"
  • Has the internet expanded the possibilities of distribution and participation, or has it undermined the tactile, material qualities that originally defined mail art?
Laurel Schwulst & Édouard U — Selections from Creative Independent and Arena: How Do You Use the Internet Mindfully

What stood out to me in Schwulst’s piece was how she describes websites with metaphors like houses, gardens, or even puddles. It made me think about the internet as something alive and constantly changing, instead of just a polished portfolio or a social media feed. Édouard takes a similar approach with reading, showing how meaning grows when we connect texts and ideas across time, instead of sticking to one linear path. Together, they made me wonder how much more personal and creative the internet could be if we treated it less like a product and more like an evolving space we can shape.

Discussion Questions
  • If we think of websites as “unfinished” and imperfect, how might that change the way we present ourselves online?
  • How could Édouard’s idea of building personal knowledge networks influence the way we study or even just use the internet in everyday life?
Olia Lialina — STILL THERE: Ruins and Templates of Geocities from Lost and Living (in) Archives

What stood out to me in Lialina’s discussion of Geocities was how the platform both democratized web creation and revealed the limits of digital permanence. The “neighborhoods” and templates she describes gave users structure, but they also reflected the constraints and assumptions built into early internet culture. I found it interesting that the survival of small artifacts like Yahoo!’s clip art or even the invisible “c.gif” carry so much historical weight, reminding us that even trivial design elements can become crucial evidence of digital heritage. It made me think about how today’s platforms, which feel so polished and seamless, might one day be studied through equally small traces.

Discussion Questions
  • In what ways does archiving digital culture differ from archiving physical culture, especially when considering scale, fragility, and accessibility?
  • What does the concept of “digital ruins” reveal about the way we experience nostalgia and memory online?
Alessandro Ludovico & Florian Cramer — Selections from Publishing from Post-Digital-Print

This reading made me think about how different print is from other media like music or video. Ludovico points out that while those formats changed carriers (CDs, MP3s, DVDs), the core experience stayed the same. But with books, the page itself is the experience, so going digital changes the experience a lot more. I thought it was interesting how print is being reimagined as something collectible or special, especially in zine culture. Cramer adds that instead of seeing print and digital as opposites, we’re in a “post-digital” world where the two overlap and mix in creative ways. Overall, these ideas made me think about how hybrid media offers new forms of participation that transcend the old “print vs. digital” binary.

Discussion Questions
  • Can print really stay independent from big tech companies, or will digital tools always pull it back into that system?
  • How does the concept of "post-digital" challenge the idea that digital media will eventually replace print entirely?
Bojana Coklyat & Shannon Finnegan — Selections from Alt-Text as Poetry Workbook

The discussion about subjectivity made me think about accessibility through a different lens. Instead of aiming for a false “objectivity,” alt-text writers are encouraged to embrace their perspectives, while still centering the needs of blind and low-vision users. This helped me see how description or alt-text can be a creative practice shaped by voice, tone, and audience, not just a technical requirement.

Discussion Questions
  • How might a writer’s identity shape the alt-text they produce?
  • Can subjectivity in alt-text actually make it more useful or meaningful to blind and low-vision readers? Why or why not?
Ramsey Nasser — "A Personal Computer for Children of All Cultures" from Decolonising the Digital

Nasser’s essay shows that programming languages and computer systems—often seen as universal—are actually rooted in English and Western assumptions. His attempt to build the Arabic programming language (Qalb) exposes how bias runs deeper than just syntax, shaping the very infrastructure of software. It brings up the question of what “accessibility” really means in tech: if coding still depends on English, who actually gets to shape the digital world?

Discussion Questions
  • How can global tech companies design tools that meaningfully include non-English languages without reinforcing existing hierarchies?
  • Is a shared “lingua franca” for programming inevitable, or should we keep pushing for culturally specific computing experiences?
David Reinfurt — Selections from A *New* Program for Graphic Design

One point that I thought was interesting from Reinfurt’s reading was his example of looking out a window and perceiving “house, sky, and trees” instead of hundreds of distinct colors. As a psych major, it was nice to see how a familiar concept (Gestalt principle of closure) can be connected to design, showing how our minds naturally simplify and organize information into coherent wholes. This concept ties closely to design, where visual elements are intentionally made to align with how we perceive the world. It’s a good reminder that strong design is grounded as much in people and psychology as it is in aesthetics.

Discussion Questions
  • How does understanding cognitive psychology, like Gestalt principles, change the way we approach visual design?
  • Reinfurt suggests we perceive wholes rather than parts; how can designers use this tendency to simplify complex information for users?
Ben Duvall — Selections from New Modernism(s)

I found Duvall’s idea of the “hyperlinked sign” really interesting—the notion that every image or symbol now carries endless layers of meaning, kind of like how links on the internet connect us to different contexts. It shows how design today relies less on clarity and more on shared digital references that not everyone might catch. It made me think about how the internet has changed not just how we design, but how we read design.

Discussion Questions
  • Do you think this layering of meaning makes design feel more open and participatory, or more confusing and exclusive?
  • If every image now carries endless layers of meaning, does this make visual communication richer or potentially more prone to misinterpretation?
Dot Tuer — Beyond the New Media Frame: The Poetics of Absence in Vera Frenkel’s String Games

One part that stuck with me was the idea of String Games creating a “poetics of absence.” I thought it was interesting how Frenkel swapped out an actual string for gestures, words, and sounds, so the whole game became more about what wasn’t there than what was. Even though everyone could see each other through the screens, there were still these little gaps or pauses that made the distance between them super noticeable. It made me think about how early this artwork recognized something we feel constantly today: that digital connection is real, but there’s always a bit of loss built into it.

Discussion Questions
  • Do you think Frenkel intended audiences in the 1970s to feel this sense of distance, or is it something we only recognize now because we’re so used to digital communication?
  • How might the experience of String Games change if it were recreated today using more immersive technology like VR?
Paul Soulellis — Performing the Feed

One part that stuck with me was Soulellis’ idea of the feed creating this “new boredom,” where we keep watching not because anything meaningful is happening, but because we know other people are watching too. It made me think about how often I scroll or watch something just for the sake of watching even if the content itself is not inherently interesting. I liked how he described the feed as a “texture,” because it honestly does feel like something I move through instead of something more intentional. It’s interesting to think about how algorithms shape everyone’s feed differently, creating unique “textures” of content, but somehow there’s still a shared feeling of collective participation.

Discussion Questions
  • If algorithms help create this constant “texture” of the feed, how does that affect our ability to tell what’s meaningful versus what’s just being pushed to us?
  • Does “watching-togetherness” actually build community, or does it just keep us passively engaged?
Boris Groys — Art on the Internet

I found Groys’ argument interesting that once art moves onto the Internet, it stops functioning as immersive fiction and instead becomes something closer to documentation or an ongoing process. The idea that art now exists alongside everyday information—rather than in a separate, protected space like a museum—made me rethink how we experience creativity online.

Discussion Questions
  • Do you think this shift toward documentation changes how meaningful or impactful art can be, or does it just reshape what we consider art in the first place?
  • How does the context of the internet change the viewer's relationship to a piece of art compared to viewing it in a separate, protected space like a museum?
